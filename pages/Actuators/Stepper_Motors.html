Stepper Motors

A stepper motor is a brushless synchronous electric motor engineered to convert digital input (pulses) into mechanical rotation. Unlike conventional electric motors, stepper motors divide a full rotation (360°) into a series of equal steps. This results in a step angle of 1.8°, which means each electrical pulse moves the shaft by exactly that amount.

Working Principle of Stepper Motor

The working principle of stepper motor is based on electromagnetic attraction. When the stator coils are energised, they produce a magnetic field that aligns the rotor with the stator pole. By energising stator coils in a specific sequence, the rotor moves incrementally from one position to the next at a specific angle. Each electrical pulse advances the rotor by one step.

Permanent Magnet Stepper Motor

A Permanent Magnet (PM) Stepper Motor is a type of stepper motor that uses a permanent magnet rotor and produces motion in discrete steps. Unlike conventional motors that rotate continuously, a stepper motor moves in precise angular steps when electrical pulses are applied. This property makes PM stepper motors highly suitable for applications requiring accurate position control without feedback systems.

Permanent magnet stepper motors are widely used in automation, instrumentation, and control systems due to their simplicity, reliability, and precise control capability.

Construction of Permanent Magnet Stepper Motor

The main components of a permanent magnet stepper motor are:

  • Stator: The stator consists of multiple windings arranged in phases. When energized sequentially, these windings produce a rotating magnetic field.
  • Rotor: The rotor is made of a permanent magnet, usually cylindrical, with north and south poles. The rotor aligns itself with the stator’s magnetic field.
  • Shaft and Bearings: The shaft is connected to the rotor and transmits mechanical motion, while bearings support smooth rotation.

The permanent magnet rotor eliminates the need for rotor windings or external excitation.

Working Principle

The working principle of a permanent magnet stepper motor is based on magnetic attraction and repulsion. When a stator winding is energized, it creates a magnetic field. The permanent magnet rotor aligns itself with this field. As different stator windings are energized in sequence, the rotor moves step by step to align with the new magnetic field position.

Each input pulse causes the motor to rotate by a fixed angle called the step angle. The direction of rotation depends on the sequence of energizing the stator windings.

Step Angle

The step angle is the angle through which the motor shaft rotates for each input pulse. It is a fixed value determined by the motor construction. Key points about step angle:

  • Smaller step angle gives higher position accuracy.
  • PM stepper motors typically have step angles such as 7.5°, 15°, or 18°.
  • Total steps per revolution = 360° / step angle.

Types of Excitation

Permanent magnet stepper motors can be operated using:

  • Single-phase excitation, where one winding is energized at a time.
  • Two-phase excitation, where two windings are energized simultaneously for higher torque.

The choice of excitation affects torque and smoothness of motion.

Applications of Permanent Magnet Stepper Motor

Permanent magnet stepper motors are commonly used in:

  • Printers and scanners.
  • Plotters and office equipment.
  • CNC positioning systems.
  • Robotics and automation equipment.
  • Instrumentation and control devices.

These applications require accurate step-by-step movement.

Hybrid Stepper Motor

A Hybrid Stepper Motor is a type of stepper motor that combines the features of Permanent Magnet (PM) stepper motors and Variable Reluctance (VR) stepper motors. This combination results in high positioning accuracy, better torque characteristics, and smooth operation. Hybrid stepper motors are the most widely used stepper motors in modern industrial automation and precision control applications.

Hybrid stepper motors move in precise, discrete steps when electrical pulses are applied, making them ideal for open-loop position control systems.

Construction of Hybrid Stepper Motor

The construction of a hybrid stepper motor includes:

  • Stator: The stator consists of multiple toothed poles with windings arranged in phases. These windings are energized in a specific sequence to create a rotating magnetic field.
  • Rotor: The rotor is made of a permanent magnet placed between two toothed iron cups. One cup has north polarity and the other has south polarity. The rotor teeth are offset slightly from each other to increase step resolution.
  • Shaft and Bearings: The rotor is mounted on a shaft supported by bearings for smooth rotation.

The combination of a permanent magnet rotor and toothed structure gives the motor high precision.

Working Principle

The working principle of a hybrid stepper motor is based on magnetic attraction and variable reluctance. When a stator phase is energized, magnetic flux is produced, and the rotor aligns itself to the position of minimum reluctance. As stator phases are energized sequentially, the rotor moves step by step.

Each input pulse rotates the shaft by a fixed angle known as the step angle. The direction of rotation depends on the order of phase excitation.

Step Angle and Resolution

Hybrid stepper motors have very small step angles, typically:

  • 1.8° per step (200 steps per revolution).
  • 0.9° per step (400 steps per revolution).

Smaller step angles provide:

  • Higher positional accuracy.
  • Smoother motion.
  • Better control at low speeds.

Microstepping techniques can further reduce the effective step angle.

Modes of Operation

Hybrid stepper motors can operate in different stepping modes:

  • Full-step mode, providing maximum torque.
  • Half-step mode, improving resolution.
  • Microstepping mode, offering very smooth and precise motion.

The mode of operation is selected based on application requirements.

Applications of Hybrid Stepper Motor

Hybrid stepper motors are widely used in:

  • CNC machines and 3D printers.
  • Robotics and automation systems.
  • Medical equipment.
  • Industrial positioning systems.
  • Precision instrumentation.

These applications demand high accuracy and reliable motion control.
